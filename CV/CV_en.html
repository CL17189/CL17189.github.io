Approaches to Key Task: Analysis of Metric Changes

When conducting data analysis in the internet industry, one common yet crucial task is performing in-depth analysis of various key metrics' fluctuations. This process helps us better understand changing trends in business operations, thereby providing more accurate support for strategic decisions. The following will delve into some of these approaches:

Metric Framework: Marketing Monitoring + Risk Management

Within our metric framework, we focus on key areas, namely marketing monitoring and risk management. This means we concentrate our study on a variety of data metrics related to marketing promotions and risk prevention. For instance, marketing aspects may encompass daily active users, conversion rates, reward amounts, and the ratio of reward issuance to Gross Merchandise Volume (GMV). On the other hand, risk management may include interception rates and loss ratios.

Segmentation Approach: Funnel with Dimensions

We adopt a funnel model combined with dimension analysis to delve more systematically into the data. For example, consider an e-commerce platform where we segment the shopping process into stages such as "product browsing," "adding to cart," "checkout," and "payment completion." For each stage, we introduce different dimensions, such as user geographical location, purchase channel, and product category, to comprehensively understand user behavior and changes.

Fluctuation Analysis Process: Precision -> Periodic Changes -> Activity Changes -> Attribution

Our fluctuation analysis aims to ensure no critical details are overlooked.

  • Accuracy Verification: Before conducting any analysis, ensuring data accuracy is paramount. For instance, if we observe a sudden increase in sales revenue, we must confirm that it is not due to errors in spreadsheets or dashboards, but rather an actual business change.
  • Identification of Periodic Changes: In metric analysis, periodic fluctuations are common, and cyclic variations should not be treated as anomalies. Moreover, for metrics with cyclical fluctuations, we often need to observe data over a longer period. For instance, a ride-hailing platform may exhibit lower activity during weekends and a noticeable surge on Mondays. Identifying and appropriately considering these cyclic changes is crucial.
  • Recognition of Activity Changes: Various business activities or holidays may lead to metric changes. Imagine an e-commerce platform hosting a promotional event, resulting in a temporary increase in sales volume. Alternatively, a fintech company might introduce a new risk assessment model, impacting loan rejection rates. Verifying these changes requires collaboration with operational stakeholders.
  • Attribution Analysis: In-depth analysis often involves considering the type of business. For mature businesses, we typically have well-established dashboards for fluctuation analysis, and following the general workflow suffices. However, for high-priority or newly acquired businesses, we need to dig deeper.

There are roughly two main approaches: dimensions and metrics.

Firstly, metrics: For composite metrics like reward issuance rate, which consists of reward amounts and GMV, we need to calculate or examine changes in these constituent metrics. This helps us understand who contributes to the overall metric change.

Secondly, dimension breakdown: Once we understand the components of metric changes, we can relate them to various dimensions. Dimensions can encompass factors that influence business, such as geographical location, product line, user type, etc. By associating metric changes with dimensions, we can reveal the extent of different dimensions' impact on metric changes, leading to a more comprehensive analysis result.
Example: After comprehending the composition of sales growth, we can link these components to different dimensions, such as city, product line, purchase channel, etc., to discern which cities or product lines contribute the most to the overall growth.

Special attention should be given to the Simpson's Paradox, a significant pitfall. For instance, while analyzing overall data, a trend in a certain dimension may emerge, but upon further segmentation, the opposite may be true. This reminds us to remain vigilant during analysis, ensuring that segmented analysis does not mask the overall trend.

Automation Tasks

Within the aforementioned process, we can establish automated tasks based on the existing approaches:

  1. Exception Handling: We can develop automated scripts to monitor unusual fluctuations in key metrics. By setting thresholds or using statistical methods, the system can automatically detect significant deviations. Once identified, the system can generate alerts and incorporate confidence level estimation, helping decision-makers better understand the severity of anomalies.
  2. Automated Data Script Generation: To accomplish metric breakdown, we can build automated data processing scripts. These scripts can extract essential information from source data and perform calculations and breakdowns based on predefined dimensions and metrics. Automated scripts can run periodically, ensuring we always have access to the latest analysis results. Additionally, these scripts can generate visualization reports for swift presentation of analysis results to decision-makers.

Building a Network: Understanding Sustainable Development Goals

Approach

Interpreting the Title: Breaking down the title, it suggests that the United Nations has established seven major development directions (primary indicators) within a document. Each of these significant indicators is evaluated or composed of several sub-indicators (secondary indicators), some of which may even have tertiary sub-indicators. The first question essentially asks us to construct a network for these seven major indicators. The subsequent questions (2-5) involve various checks or refinements for this established network's properties, robustness, and completeness.

At this point, the foremost challenge is how to establish this network and what kind of network to create. Commonly known network models include Bayesian networks, Social Network Analysis (SNA), causal diagrams, and more. These network models typically share features such as nodes and edges, with edges having attributes like length and direction. Nodes could represent probabilities, quantities, and more. Hence, the answer to "how to establish this network" primarily starts with quantification. In other words, we need to acquire data involving these seven indicators and their underlying sub-indicators for a certain country over a period. Luckily, we have found this data source: United Nations surveys.

The data source provides data values for over a hundred secondary indicators for a specific country spanning from 2011 to 2020. As diligent statisticians, we naturally proceed with data cleansing, handling missing values, and visualization. The next question is, how do these hundred-plus secondary indicators relate to the seven major indicators? This step is relatively simple; we can directly employ an aggregation model to weight the sub-indicators and synthesize primary indicators. We've chosen the entropy weight method for aggregation, as its objectivity makes it a suitable choice. After applying the entropy weight method post normalizing with the max-min technique, we have the values for the seven major indicators (nodes), but how do we determine the edges? Pearl's book on linear models provided an enlightening perspective – what's a network, what's an indicator being achieved? Isn't it just a causal graph and a do-operator? The edge length, i.e., the quantification of the relationship between two indicators, can be represented by the coefficient of partial regression.

With edges and nodes quantified, what model should be used? Here, we introduce the first assumption of this paper: these seven indicators mutually influence each other – as various factors in social life are interconnected. Pay attention to the keywords "mutual" and "influence." Drawing inspiration from Bayesian networks, we introduce the notion of one factor influencing another as an arrow from one node to another (familiar to those acquainted with causal diagrams). Therefore, this network, considering all the conditions, should be a cyclic bidirectional graph, aligning with the structure of an SNA network.

In the third step, we refine the network. Do all pairs of factors necessarily have a relationship? Not necessarily. How can statistical methods determine whether a relationship is "significant"? In linear models, we usually perform t-tests on regression coefficients or employ F-tests to assess the model. Since we hadn't considered linear models at that point, we explored an alternative direction – time series analysis. Granger causality tests in multivariate time series help determine the significance of Granger causal relationships between weakly stationary sequences. However, in our data, almost no series was stationary, and the time period was limited. So, we adopted another method, particularly effective for assessing causal relationships between more general sequences – transfer entropy tests. This approach even handles nonlinear relationships effectively. It assesses the difference between the conditional entropy and the original entropy value for a sequence given the existence of another sequence, termed transfer entropy. Having this criterion, we can identify which pairs of sequences have insignificant causal relationships and consequently remove those edges from our network. Finally, our network is constructed.

Firstly, we compute the importance indices for each node in the network. Our idea is that if an indicator is sufficiently significant, it should be linked to more nodes. Thus, we employ certain centrality indices from graph theory to gauge the significance of each factor. Metrics like out-degree, in-degree, and other indices mentioned in literature are used. Based on these measurements, we identify the top four Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) for prioritization. Subsequently, our team employs PCA to extract primary components from these four priority SDGs and validates their effectiveness using linear regression models.
